Visual Brand Guidelines

A complete visual canon for agents, designers, and AI tools producing creative for Jay Trainer Music. Specific enough to be an operating manual. Built to prevent generic output and protect creative integrity.

Version 1.0  ·  May 2026  ·  Briar Patch Records
Section 01

Core Visual Identity

What Jay Trainer should feel like before it looks like anything. These are the emotional briefs that govern every creative decision downstream.

The Feeling Brief

You are sitting in a room where someone recently played guitar. The amp is still warm. There is a coffee ring on a lyric sheet. Outside the window is a Pittsburgh sky in November — neither romantic nor grim, just real. The music playing back on a rough phone recording sounds better than it has any right to.

This is the emotional center of Jay Trainer's brand. Lived-in. Unsentimental. Quietly serious. The work speaks because it was earned. The visuals must earn their place the same way — through specificity, not style.

Brand Personality in Three Words

Pillar 01
Grit

Authentic, unpolished, human. The process shows. The calluses show. No smoke machines, no aspirational skylines.

Pillar 02
Soul

Emotionally resonant, warm but dark. The lyricism is stark. Visuals should earn silence, not fill it.

Pillar 03
Cosmic

Psychedelic depth. Not sci-fi. Not festival. The infinite sustain of a well-placed fuzz pedal — uncontrolled beauty within tight craft.

What It Is Not

Generic Americana: No barn doors, no wheat fields, no Route 66 road signs, no mason jars, no flannel-and-denim pose shots, no sepia Instagram "vintage" filter on a guitar lying in grass. These are aesthetic shortcuts that say nothing about this music specifically.
Music Industry Cliché: No crowd shots with phone lights, no studio-montage clichés (headphones around neck, pointing at screen), no album release announcement graphics that look like Spotify playlist covers. No gradient text. No neon.
Clean Digital Perfection: This brand is not polished. Heavily retouched photography, 4K-sharp digital renders, and stock-feeling composites are off-brand. The standard is analog warmth — grain, weight, wear.

The Pittsburgh Specificity Rule

Pittsburgh is not a backdrop. It is a material. Wet steel. River-grey skies. Bridge geometry. The smell of old rehearsal rooms on the South Side. Brick walls with paint palimpsests. If "Pittsburgh" could be swapped out for "Nashville" or "Brooklyn" without changing the image, it is not specific enough.

The Catalog Gravity Rule

Jay Trainer has a deep catalog spanning Blackout Asylum, A Whisper Of Ruin, Den Of Thieves, Of The Sun, The Waking Hours, and "Lifeline." The visual brand must carry that weight. It cannot look like a debut act. It must look like someone who has been making serious records for years and has the rooms to prove it.

Section 02

Logo & Wordmark

The calligraphic wordmark is the only logo. It cannot be recreated in type, substituted with another font, or generated by AI. It is a handcrafted artifact — treat it accordingly.

Primary wordmark  ·  Light on Coal  ·  The only approved version for dark surfaces

Color Variants

There are exactly four approved color treatments. No others are permitted.

01 — White on Coal

Primary. Dark backgrounds: page headers, stage graphics, album art on dark ground, social post templates, merchandise on black.

02 — White 78% on Smoke

Social assets. Used on Screen Print and Psychedelic Collage pillars. Slightly dimmed so the type reads as a brand mark, not a foreground element competing with the main visual.

03 — Coal 65% on Parchment

Light surfaces: lyric cards, press quotes, liner-note insets, newsletter headers, Record Sleeve social pillar. The original dark fill at reduced opacity to prevent harshness against warm paper.

04 — Black 55% on Ember

Accent use only. Type Monument amber pillar, limited-edition print, secondary merchandise colourway. Never used as the primary brand presentation.

Clear Space Rule

The wordmark requires a minimum clear zone on all sides equal to the cap-height of the letter J (approximately 1/5 of the logo height). Nothing — text, images, page edges, other logos — may enter this zone.

Below 120px wide the calligraphic letterforms degrade. At 80px and below the wordmark becomes illegible. Never use below 120px on screen; never below 30mm in print.

Never Do This

These are not guidelines. They are prohibitions. Any asset violating them must be rejected.

No stretching
Never alter the aspect ratio of the wordmark. The calligraphic proportions are fixed. Scaling is uniform only.
No recreation in type
Never attempt to replicate the wordmark using any typeface, including script fonts. There is no typographic substitute. If the SVG is unavailable, use no logo.
No color outside the four variants
Do not colorize the logo in brand colors (amber, rust) or any other color. The four approved treatments are white-on-dark, white-dimmed, coal-on-parchment, and black-on-amber.
No drop shadows or glows
Never add drop shadows, outer glows, inner glows, emboss effects, or any layer style to the wordmark. Legibility is achieved through background contrast alone.
No rotation
The logo runs horizontal. It is never tilted, angled, or placed on a curve. On merchandise, it is level.
No AI generation of the logo
Never ask an AI image tool to generate, recreate, or approximate the Jay Trainer wordmark. AI generation will produce a plausible-looking fake. The SVG file is the only source.
No placement on busy backgrounds
The logo must always have sufficient contrast. On photography, use a vignette, overlay, or solid zone behind the wordmark. Never place it on a mid-tone busy image without contrast treatment.
No minimum size violations
Never render the logo below 120px wide on screen or 30mm in print. At small sizes, use the wordmark as a text credit only — never as a visual focal point.
File source: The authoritative wordmark file is assets/jaytrainer-logo-v1.svg. It is an SVG with a fixed viewBox of 2771×790. All agents must reference this path. Never re-export, re-trace, or re-render from a screenshot or raster image.
Section 03

Reference Library Requirements

What must be collected, organized, and made available to every creative agent. References are the difference between good art direction and generic output.

Category A — Primary Jay Trainer Assets

These are non-negotiable. Every AI generation pipeline must have access to these before producing any output.

A1 · Jay Photos
Artist Photography

Performance shots, studio sessions, behind-the-scenes. Minimum 20 approved images in varied lighting. Required: at least 5 with guitar in hand, 3 close-up/portrait, 3 full-body stage shots. Risk: Never use these as AI training references to generate fake likenesses.

A2 · Album Covers
Real Album Art

All six releases as high-res reference. Study the color temperature, type treatment, and compositional logic of each. Use to understand how each era looked — not to copy, but to maintain coherence.

A3 · Gear
Real Instruments & Equipment

Jay's specific guitars, amps, pedal board (with fuzz and wah prominently), microphones, and cables. These objects are characters in the visual narrative. Risk: Generic guitar stock images immediately read as fake.

A4 · Spaces
Real Rooms & Venues

Rehearsal room, home recording space, Pittsburgh venues where Jay plays regularly. These are the settings. Risk: Never imply large arenas, sold-out shows, or crowds that don't exist.

A5 · Handwritten Materials
Lyric Sheets & Notebooks

Real lyric sheets, chord charts, notebook pages in Jay's actual handwriting. These are irreplaceable brand artifacts. Photographed flat or in situ.

A6 · Pittsburgh Textures
Local Material Library

Bridge steel, river surfaces, brick walls, wet pavement, wood grain, industrial windows. Shot in Pittsburgh. Specifically: the Mon, the 40th Street Bridge, South Side streets, winter light on water.

Category B — Outside Visual References (20–40 images)

These should be collected as a private Moodboard folder. Each reference must be tagged with what it contributes and what to avoid borrowing too directly.

Approved reference directions and their purpose

  • Mark Seliger / Danny Clinch portrait work: Grain, weight, natural light, the subject being real rather than posed. Not the celebrity staging — the intimacy of the best frames.
  • ECM Records album art (1970s–1990s): Typographic austerity, open space, photography that breathes. The cold-but-emotional quality of Manfred Eicher's label aesthetic.
  • Robert Frank's "The Americans" (1958): Documentary grain, American mundanity elevated, the unflattering made honest. Use for spatial language, not subject matter.
  • Ryan Adams' "Heartbreaker" / "Gold" era press photography: Singer-songwriter in real spaces, natural light, guitar as natural extension of the body.
  • Jim Marshall's concert photography: Stage light carving through dark rooms. Heat and electricity in still images.
  • Vintage Blue Note Records sleeves (1950s–60s): Type-as-image, geometric composition, bold two-color printing, real photographs not stock.
  • Beggars Group / 4AD art direction (1980s): Texture, collage, the physical process of making a printed object. Applicable to album art and poster work.
  • Anders Petersen photography: Intimacy, bar rooms, late-night proximity. Not appropriated literally — use to understand spatial and emotional closeness.
  • Nan Goldin's "The Ballad of Sexual Dependency": Color temperature of real rooms, the non-performance of real life. Applies to environment references.
  • Gary Clark Jr. press photography: Contemporary analog feel, guitar-centric, urban-rooted without being generic urban.

What to never reference

Never reference: Mumford & Sons-era folk aesthetic · Generic Spotify editorial photography · "Indie band in field" shoots · Taylor Swift's vintage aesthetic (too clean, too feminine) · Ed Sheeran stage photography (too stadium) · Stock music photography of any kind · AI-generated "guitarist" images from Midjourney defaults · Any imagery suggesting religious iconography (misreads the lyrical themes) · Heavy metal / stoner rock visual language (wrong genre register) · Hip-hop visual references (wrong cultural context)

Section 04

Creative Territories

Six repeatable visual lanes. Each is a complete creative system with its own emotional logic, technical parameters, and example prompts. Use exactly one territory per post or asset — do not blend.

01 — Weathered Lyric Sheet
The intimacy of the writing process. Words before they became music.

Emotional purpose

  • Brings the audience inside the creative act
  • Communicates that lyrics are literature, not filler
  • Creates intimacy without performance

Best use cases

  • Lyric posts and quote graphics
  • Song announcement posts
  • Behind-the-scenes social content
  • Website lyric section headers

Approved subjects

  • Real handwritten lyric pages — Jay's actual writing
  • Coffee rings, pen marks, strike-throughs
  • A single guitar string or pick resting on paper
  • Aged paper on wood table with afternoon window light

Forbidden subjects

  • Typed or printed lyrics (must be handwritten)
  • Fake handwriting fonts as substitutes
  • Multiple items cluttering the frame
  • Overly styled flat lays
  • Any person visible in frame

Lighting

  • Raking natural light from one side — morning or late afternoon
  • Warm, never flash-lit or clinical
  • Some areas may fall into shadow

Color palette

  • Warm cream/parchment paper
  • Amber light on surface edges
  • Deep shadow fills — near coal
  • Occasional blue-grey of afternoon light

Texture requirements

  • Paper grain must be visible
  • Ink must look real — slight bleed, not vector-clean
  • Wood grain visible in background
  • 15–20% grain overlay in final output

Camera / framing language

  • Close-up, slightly overhead — 60° angle
  • Shallow depth of field if letter edges go soft
  • Do not center perfectly — slight off-center tension

Dark-first rule: The default canvas is Ink (#111110). Light surfaces (Parchment) appear only for lyric cards, press quotes, and liner-note-style insets. Never use pure white (#ffffff) as a background.

Gradient rule: Only black-to-transparent vignettes over photography. No color-to-color gradients. No purple-to-pink. No glow effects.

Typography Direction

Display

Fraunces (variable, SOFT + WONK axes available). Used for primary titles, album names, pull quotes. Weight 700+. Letter-spacing tight at −0.02em to −0.04em for large sizes. Not for body text.

Accent Script

Jeff Dungan (licensed). Used sparingly — song titles in script treatment, era labels, single-word emotional accents. Never for full sentences. Never for UI labels.

Utility / Mono

Space Mono. Track listings, timestamps, metadata, catalog numbers, file names, captions. All uppercase with generous letter-spacing (0.15–0.20em). This is the gritty-technical voice of the brand.

Body

Inter (or Lora for print/press contexts). 14px minimum on screen. Line height 1.7 for body copy. Never condensed, never below 14px.

Layout Rules

DOUse asymmetric composition — the interesting tension is always off-center
DOAllow generous negative space — silence is part of this brand
DOBleed photography to edges — do not box images in decorative frames
NOCentered layouts with perfectly equal margins — too inert
NODrop shadows on text — use color contrast or background for legibility
NORounded corners greater than 4px on primary design elements
NOGrid-aligned stacks of equal-sized elements — introduce scale variation

Photography Style

  • Color grading: warm analog — amber highlights, shadows crushed to near-black, not lifted
  • Grain: always present — 15–25% noise at output. No clean digital photographs.
  • Faces: close-up portraits must have depth, shadow, and weight. Not beauty-retouched.
  • Environments: photographed as-found. No art-directed set dressing unless it is real.
  • B&W: acceptable for press photos — prefer warm sepia lean over cold grey.

Social Media Rules

  • Use the four established pillars: Screen Print, Type Monument, Record Sleeve, Psychedelic Collage
  • Never repeat the same pillar on consecutive days
  • Every post carries the Jay Trainer calligraphic logo — always visible, never obscured
  • No emoji in captions. No exclamation points. No "just dropped" language.
  • Caption voice: first person, sparse. 1–3 sentences maximum. 5–8 relevant hashtags.
  • No fake engagement tactics — no "what's your favorite song?" content farm questions

Album Art Rules

  • Must reference one of the six Creative Territories — no free-floating concept
  • Photography-based preferred over illustration
  • No stock photography under any circumstances
  • Type treatment must use approved typefaces — no display font outside the system
  • Minimum one analog texture layer (grain, foxing, tanning, water stain)
  • Square format primary; vertical variants derived from square, not designed separately

Lyric Video Rules

  • Background must be one of the six Creative Territories in motion
  • Lyric type: Fraunces for display words, Space Mono for metadata (time, track number)
  • Text entry: cut, fade, or slow push — never wipe, bounce, or karaoke highlight
  • No animated text effects — the word arrives, holds, and leaves
  • Duration: one visual territory per section (verse, chorus, bridge)
  • Grain overlay required throughout — minimum 12% opacity

Music Video Rules

  • See Section 08 — Video Direction for full specification
  • No fake crowds, no fake venues, no implied commercial success
  • Documentary or implied-documentary aesthetic only
  • If Jay appears, use real photography or real footage only — never AI-generated likeness
Section 06

AI Generation Rules

The discipline required to get AI tools to produce specific, honest output instead of confident-looking garbage.

Prompt Architecture

Every AI image prompt must follow this structure, in order:

Prompt structure template
[SHOT TYPE] + [PRIMARY SUBJECT from approved list] + [ENVIRONMENT specifics] + [LIGHTING description] + [COLOR PALETTE] + [FILM/TEXTURE style] + [EMOTIONAL TONE] + "NOT [top 3 negatives]"

Required Prompt Ingredients

Every prompt must include at minimum:

  • Shot type: close-up, wide angle, overhead, from behind, etc.
  • Specific subject: not "guitar" — "guitar leaning against amp with cable coiled, power light on"
  • Lighting description: not "moody" — "raking afternoon light from single window left, deep shadow right"
  • Film reference: "35mm grain," "analog film photography," "Kodak Portra 400," "Fuji Superia 1600"
  • At least one negative qualifier appended at end: "NOT stock, NOT clean, NOT digital render"

Master Negative Prompt Library

Always include relevant items from this list. More negatives = more specific output.

How to Avoid Generic AI Aesthetics

The AI default problem: When you type "guitarist in a dark room," every AI model has been trained on millions of near-identical images and will give you its statistical average. To escape: be specific to a degree that the AI has no average to fall back on. "Worn Telecaster-style guitar leaning against a vintage Fender Deluxe Reverb amp with the pilot light on, seen from floor level, afternoon light through venetian blinds making horizontal stripes across the amp grille cloth" produces something different.
  • Specify exact angles that are unusual — from floor level looking up, from directly above, from behind a foreground object
  • Include imperfections in the prompt: "scratch on guitar body," "coffee ring on lyric sheet," "worn knob on amp"
  • Reference specific film stocks and photographers — not as copyright claims, but as aesthetic anchors
  • Generate in batches of 6–10 and select the least AI-looking option
  • Reject anything with smooth gradients in unexpected places, plastic-looking surfaces, or mathematically perfect symmetry

What Must Never Be Implied Visually

Hard prohibitions — any image doing these must be rejected immediately:
  • Fake crowds or audiences implying a scale of success not documented
  • Fake live footage or implication of real concert footage when it is AI-generated
  • Jay Trainer's likeness generated by AI — his face, body, or identifiable appearance
  • Fake press clippings, fake chart positions, fake award designations
  • Fake tour posters implying past tours that did not happen
  • Venue names implying past shows at venues not played
  • Any implied endorsement — gear companies, media outlets, other artists

Batch Generation Workflow

  1. Define territory — which of the 6 Creative Territories is this for?
  2. Write base prompt using the structure template above
  3. Generate 8–12 variants — vary seed, not the core prompt
  4. First pass filter: reject anything with person/crowd/award/AI-smooth surfaces immediately
  5. Score remaining against Quality Rubric (Section 07)
  6. Minimum threshold: 4/5 on Brand Fit, all three Pass/Fail criteria must pass
  7. Upscale only approved items — do not upscale for review; upscale for delivery
  8. Move to C-approved-outputs/ with file naming per Section 10

Quality Rubric

Every image and video must be scored before use. Minimum approval: 4/5 on Brand Fit, 3/5 on all other scored criteria, all three pass/fail criteria must pass.

Image Quality AI artifacts visible: smooth gradients where grain expected, warped text, plastic surfaces. Mostly believable. Slight AI smoothness but not distracting. Indistinguishable from analog photography. Grain is correct. Surfaces have weight.

Section 08

Video Direction

Real AI video is built from short approved shots, not from a single long generation. The slideshow era is over. This is the production standard.

The Shot Unit

The fundamental unit of AI video production for Jay Trainer is a 3–5 second approved shot. Each shot belongs to exactly one Creative Territory. Each shot passes the Quality Rubric. A complete visual packet for one song = 12–30 approved shots.

Shot Language Reference

Shot Type 01
Static Hold

Camera locked. Subject still or barely moving — amp power light on, steam from coffee, rain falling. Hold 3–4 seconds. Used for atmospheric transitions.

Shot Type 02
Slow Push

Camera moves toward subject at glacial speed over 4–5 seconds. Creates presence and weight. Not a zoom — a physical movement through space.

Shot Type 03
Overhead Float

Looking straight down at a surface — lyric sheet, pedal board, record sleeves. Camera drifts slightly. Never rotates.

Shot Type 04
Raking Pull

Camera moves slowly along a surface — across a wall, along a bridge, across a floor. Reveals texture. Creates rhythm.

Shot Type 05
Light Event

Pure atmosphere. A door opening and light entering a dark room. A lamp switching on. A car headlight crossing a wet surface. 3 seconds maximum.

Technical Parameters

  • Grain: Film grain overlay required on all video — minimum 18% opacity, variable slightly to avoid uniform pattern
  • Color: Warm grade — shadows pushed deep, highlights slightly amber, not crushed into black (retain shadow detail)
  • Frame rate: 24fps. Never 60fps. Never slow-motion unless real — AI-generated slow motion creates unnatural motion blur
  • Transitions: Hard cut or slow dissolve (0.5–1s) only. No wipes, no slide, no zoom-transition effects
  • Aspect ratio: 16:9 for YouTube primary. 9:16 variants cut from approved 16:9 shots — subject must be center-weighted to survive the crop

Title and Lyric Treatment in Video

  • Song title: Fraunces, weight 700, center or lower-third. Fade in over 0.5s, hold for full read, fade out 0.5s. Never animated beyond opacity.
  • Lyric lines: Fraunces italic, white on dark sections. Space Mono for metadata. One line at a time — never paragraph blocks.
  • Artist name: Jeff Dungan script or the logo SVG. Lower-left or lower-center. Always present in the final card.
  • No lower-third bar graphics. No social media handle overlays mid-video. Credit card at end only.

Assembling a Music Video from 12–30 Shots

  1. Map shots to song structure: Intro (2–3 shots), verse 1 (3–4 shots), chorus 1 (2–3 shots), verse 2 (3–4 shots), chorus 2 (2–3 shots), bridge (2 shots), outro (2–3 shots)
  2. Territory assignment: Intro and outro = same territory. Verse = one territory. Chorus = different territory for contrast. Bridge = most abstract territory.
  3. Pacing: Cuts on musical phrases, not on every beat. The edits should feel late — resist the urge to cut on every bar
  4. End card: 5-second hold on logo + song title + "Stream now" + link. Black background. No animation.

What Must Never Be Implied in Video

Hard video prohibitions: No fake live performance footage (not AI-generated crowd shots with music) · No AI-generated likeness of Jay Trainer in video · No implied documentary footage when it is AI-generated · No fake backstage, fake tour, fake press appearance footage · No music video that implies a real event that did not occur · No crowd singing along to a song

Governance

How creative assets move from generation to approval to publication. Clear ownership, clear file structure, no ambiguity.

Approval Workflow

StatusDefinitionWho can move it forward
GeneratedRaw AI output. Not scored. Not reviewed. Held in generation folder.Any agent can generate. No publishing rights.
DraftScored against rubric. Meets minimum threshold (Brand Fit ≥ 4, all Pass/Fail passed). Caption written.Campaign manager or AI agent with rubric access.

RejectedFailed rubric or Jay review. Moved to X-rejected/ with rejection note.Any reviewer. Rejection note required.

What Requires Jay Approval

  • Any asset featuring a person — even silhouette or from behind
  • Any asset that references a specific venue, event, or date
  • Any asset used in paid promotion or advertising
  • Album art and single cover finals
  • Any caption containing first-person statements (Jay's voice)
  • Music video assemblies — final cut before upload
  • Any asset that references another artist, label, or publication

What Can Move Forward Without Jay Approval

  • Abstract environment shots (no people, no location claims)
  • Texture and gear-object photography (amp, pedal, lyric sheet without words)
  • Standard pillar posts from the 2-Week Campaign template where all assets were pre-approved
  • Reposting of previously approved assets in new format sizes

File / Folder Naming Convention

Standard file name format
[YYYYMMDD]_[TERRITORY-CODE]_[SONG-SLUG]_[PLATFORM]_[STATUS].[ext] Examples: 20260527_SP_lifeline_IG-FEED_APPROVED.jpg 20260528_RS_notmycrosstobear_9x16_DRAFT.jpg 20260529_TM_ofthesun_YT-COMMUNITY_APPROVED.jpg 20260530_PC_lifeline_ALBUM-ART_REJECTED.jpg Territory codes: SP=Screen Print, RS=Record Sleeve, TM=Type Monument, PC=Psychedelic Collage WT=Weathered Lyric Sheet, SS=Small Stage, PR=Pittsburgh River, RH=Rehearsal Room, RA=Road After Rain, AC=Album Collage

Manifest Requirements

Every approved asset must have a manifest entry containing: file path, territory code, song associated, platform, dimensions, rubric scores, approval date, approver, and any usage restrictions. The manifest is the authoritative record of what exists and where it is cleared for use.

How AI Agents Should Use These Guidelines

  1. Before generating any visual, identify the Creative Territory from Section 04
  2. Build the prompt using the structure from Section 06
  3. Include all relevant items from the Master Negative Prompt Library
  4. Generate a batch — never use the first output
  5. Score each output against the rubric in Section 07
  6. Present only Draft-status items for Jay's review
  7. Store all generated items in the correct folder per the naming convention
  8. Never publish without explicit Approved status in the manifest
